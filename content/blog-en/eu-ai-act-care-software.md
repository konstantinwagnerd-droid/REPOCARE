## What this is about

The EU Regulation 2024/1689 — the AI Act — has been in force since August 2024. It applies in stages through August 2026. For care facilities and software vendors the practical question is: **does our care software become a high-risk system, and what does that mean?**

The short answer: yes, parts of care software fall into the high-risk category — but the requirements are manageable. Panic is misplaced; ignorance equally so.

## The four risk classes

The AI Act distinguishes:

1. **Unacceptable risk (forbidden)**: social scoring, emotional manipulation. Not relevant for care.
2. **High risk (regulated)**: biometric identification, access to essential services, healthcare.
3. **Limited risk (transparency duties)**: e.g. chatbots — the person must know they are talking to AI.
4. **Minimal risk (free)**: spam filters, recommendation engines.

## Where does care software sit?

It depends on the **function** of the AI module. Voice-to-text transcription alone is usually limited risk. Predictive risk scoring for falls, delirium, pressure ulcers is high risk — it informs clinical decisions.

## What providers need to do

- Document which AI modules are in use and their classification
- Ensure human oversight is enabled by default (CareAI: on by default)
- Keep audit logs for at least 6 months post-decision
- Train staff on AI limitations (30 minutes suffices for most modules)
